---
title: Guidelines for Posting
description: Guidelines to follow before posting to chat.
---

These guidelines exist not to silence anyone, but to call us each to the standard we have already set for ourselves as New Warriors. This is a chat group – not a circle – but the same values apply.

### Speak from your own experience

Use "I" statements. Share what *you* feel, what *you* have witnessed, what *you* believe. Speak from the inside out.

- *"I feel angry when I read this, and I notice I want to respond strongly"* – rather than *"You are being divisive."*
- *"I find myself triggered by this topic"* – rather than *"This is offensive."*
- *"In my experience..."* – rather than *"Everyone knows that..."*

Owning your experience is not weakness. It is the foundation of authentic communication.

### Name your feelings, not just your opinions

The New Warrior knows what he feels. A post that says *"I'm scared about what's happening in the world"* opens a conversation. A post that says *"The world is going to hell"* closes it. Feelings invite connection; declarations invite debate.

Where possible, locate the feeling beneath the opinion and lead with that.

### Own your judgements

We all make judgements. The Clearing process teaches us to name them honestly: *"I see you as..."* followed by the capacity to own those judgements as part of ourselves.

On chat, this means: if you are judging another man, a group, or a position, be willing to say so directly and in the first person. *"I judge this as dishonest"* is cleaner and more respectful than wrapping a judgement in the language of objective fact.

Ask yourself: *What does this judgement tell me about myself?* That is often where the more interesting work lies.

### Distinguish data from interpretation

Before posting, try to separate:

- **Data** – what actually happened, what was actually said (as a camera would record it)
- **Judgement** – your interpretation of that data
- **Feeling** – your emotional response

Mixing these together without labelling them creates confusion and conflict. Keeping them distinct creates clarity.

### Avoid shaming, labelling, and diagnosing

*"You clearly need to do your shadow work"*, *"Shame on anyone who..."*, *"That's just woke ideology"* – these phrases shut conversation down. They place us above the other man, which is contrary to our values: *never above you, never below you, always by your side.*

If you believe a brother has something to look at, the gift of accountability is offered cleanly, without charge, and without attachment to whether he takes it up.

### Disagree without dismissing

Men will hold genuinely different views on politics, society, and the world. That is not a problem to be solved – it is a reality to be respected. You can hold your position firmly and still hold the other man as a brother.

If someone's view challenges you, consider: *What can I be curious about here?* Curiosity is not agreement. It is a mark of a mature masculine man.

### Speak to the topic, not the man

Challenge ideas, question arguments, share counter-experience. But do not attack the man himself. Personal insults, sarcasm, and condescension have no place here, regardless of how strongly you feel about the topic.

### Less is more

A short, honest, first-person post almost always lands better than a long argument. If you find yourself writing at length to *win* rather than to *share*, that is a signal worth heeding.

### If the heat is too high

If a thread has escalated to the point where you cannot engage from a grounded place, it is entirely appropriate – and indeed a warrior's move – to step back. You can say: *"I'm feeling too charged on this to contribute helpfully right now."*

You might also use this as an invitation to bring the topic into a circle, a PIT, or an I-Group, where it can be held with proper support.

---

## A Final Word

This chat is part of a community built on the belief that the world needs good men – grounded, accountable, emotionally literate, and connected to one another. When we post from that place, we serve that mission. When we do not, we undermine it.

The work we do on ourselves in circles and trainings is not separate from how we show up in this chat. It is an extension of it.

*In service.*
